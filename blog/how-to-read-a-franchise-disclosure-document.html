How to Read a Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD)

FranchiseDealBreaker April 2026 14 min read

A Franchise Disclosure Document is a legally required document that every franchisor must give you at least 14 days before you sign a franchise agreement or pay any money. It can run 200 to 500 pages. Most prospective franchise buyers read about 30 pages of it before their eyes glaze over.

That's how people lose $300,000.

This guide will walk you through exactly how to read an FDD — not every page, but the items that actually matter. The items where franchisors hide bad news. The items that predict whether you'll make money or lose your shirt.

⚠ Important

This guide is for educational purposes and does not constitute legal advice. Before signing any franchise agreement, consult a licensed franchise attorney. The FDD analysis here is general — your specific FDD may have unique risks.

What Is an FDD, Exactly?

The FTC Franchise Rule requires every franchisor selling franchises in the United States to prepare a Franchise Disclosure Document and deliver it to prospective franchisees. The FDD must be updated annually and must include 23 specific items of information — all defined by the FTC.

The FDD is not the franchise agreement. The franchise agreement is the contract you sign. The FDD is the disclosure document that gives you information to make an informed decision. You should read both, but the FDD is where the red flags hide.

You have a legal right to receive the FDD at least 14 days before signing anything. Any franchisor who pressures you to sign before that window is violating federal law. Walk away.

The 23 Items — and Which Ones Actually Matter

Every FDD has 23 required items. Most of them are routine disclosure boilerplate. Six of them will tell you most of what you need to know. Here they are in order of importance:

ITEM 19 — HIGH PRIORITY

Financial Performance Representations

This is the most important item in the FDD. If the franchisor makes any claims about how much money you can make, those claims must appear here. If Item 19 is missing — or if it only shows top-quartile earners — that's a major red flag. Many franchisors disclose only a sliver of favorable data here.

ITEM 3 — HIGH PRIORITY

Litigation History

Every lawsuit, arbitration, and regulatory action involving the franchisor and its officers must be disclosed here. Volume matters: a handful of routine actions is normal for a 5,000-unit system. A pattern of franchisee-initiated lawsuits about earnings misrepresentation is not. Read every entry and look for patterns.

ITEM 20 — HIGH PRIORITY

Outlets and Franchisee Information

This item shows you how many locations opened, closed, and transferred in the past three years. High closure rates are the most objective signal of franchisee failure in the entire document. If 10% of locations closed last year, that's not bad luck — that's a broken business model.

ITEM 21 — HIGH PRIORITY

Financial Statements

Three years of audited financial statements for the franchisor itself. You want to know if the parent company is financially healthy. A franchisor teetering on bankruptcy cannot support its franchise network. Look for consistent losses, declining revenue, or heavy debt loads.

ITEM 12 — HIGH PRIORITY

Territory

What territory protection are you actually getting? Many franchise agreements offer far less protection than what was described during the sales process. Are there carve-outs for online sales? Can the franchisor open company-owned locations in your market? Can they sell to customers in your territory through other channels?

ITEM 17 — HIGH PRIORITY

Renewal, Termination, Transfer, and Dispute Resolution

What happens when things go wrong? Under what conditions can the franchisor terminate your agreement? What are your renewal rights? If you want to sell your franchise, what fees and approval processes apply? The answers here determine how much control you actually have over your own business.

How to Read Item 19 — The Most Important Item

Item 19 is where most prospective franchisees get deceived — not through lies, but through selective disclosure. Here's how to read it correctly.

First, check if Item 19 exists at all.

Franchisors are not legally required to make financial performance representations. Many don't. If Item 19 says something like "We do not make any representations about a franchisee's future financial performance," that's your answer: the franchisor is not willing to stand behind any earnings numbers.

That doesn't necessarily mean you shouldn't buy the franchise. But it means you're going in blind on financials, which changes your risk profile significantly.

If Item 19 exists, look at what's being disclosed.

Some franchisors disclose average gross revenue. Some disclose median gross revenue. Some disclose only top-quartile performers. Some show all three. The more complete the disclosure, the more trustworthy it is.

What you really want to see: net income or owner earnings data. Gross revenue means nothing without knowing what it costs to generate it. A location doing $800,000 in gross revenue with $750,000 in expenses is a bad investment. Always ask existing franchisees what their actual net income looks like.

Check the sample size.

If a system has 1,200 locations but Item 19 only covers data from 340 of them, ask why. Are the missing 860 locations underperforming? Did some not provide data? Was data excluded for any reason? Small samples in large systems are a yellow flag.

How to Read Item 3 — Litigation History

Reading Item 3 requires some context. A large franchise system with 5,000 locations will have more disclosed litigation than a small system with 200 locations — that's just math. What you're looking for is patterns.

Normal litigation includes routine commercial disputes, slip-and-fall liability at franchise locations, employment matters, and supplier disagreements. These are table stakes for operating a large retail business.

Red flag litigation includes: multiple franchisees suing over earnings misrepresentations, class action lawsuits from franchisees alleging systemic fraud, regulatory actions from state attorneys general, and personal litigation involving the founders or officers.

When you see franchisee-initiated lawsuits alleging that they were misled about earnings or territory, pay close attention. This is a pattern that often repeats.

How to Read Item 20 — The Closure Rate

Item 20 contains one of the most objective data points in the entire FDD: how many locations closed last year. Here's how to calculate a meaningful closure rate:

  • Find the total number of franchised locations at the start of the year (Column A)
  • Find the number of locations that closed, were terminated, or were not renewed (Column B)
  • Divide B by A. That's your annual closure rate.

A closure rate under 2% is healthy. 2-5% is worth investigating. Over 5% is a serious red flag that demands explanation. Over 10% means the business model is in crisis.

Also look at transfers. High transfer rates can indicate franchisees who want out of their agreements. When franchisees are selling at a high rate, ask why — and talk to the franchisees who sold.

How to Read Item 21 — Franchisor Financial Health

Three years of audited financial statements sounds daunting. You don't need to be an accountant to get what you need from this section. Here's what to look for:

  • Is the franchisor profitable? Look at net income on the income statement. If the franchisor is consistently losing money, that's a concern — though some younger brands run at a loss during growth phases.
  • Is revenue growing or declining? Consistent revenue growth indicates a healthy, expanding system. Revenue declines may indicate struggling unit economics or same-store sales pressure.
  • How much debt does the franchisor carry? Look at the balance sheet. A heavily leveraged parent company is vulnerable to economic downturns in ways that can hurt franchisees.

If the franchisor is a subsidiary of a larger company (as many franchise brands are), look at the parent company's financials as well. The parent's health matters as much as the franchisor subsidiary's health.

The 5 Questions to Ask Every Existing Franchisee

The FDD is a legal document written by lawyers. It will tell you what the franchisor is required to tell you — no more. The real intelligence comes from talking to existing franchisees. Item 20 includes a list of every current franchisee and their contact information. Call them.

  1. "What does your location actually net after all expenses?" This is the question that separates real buyers from tire-kickers. Most franchisees will answer honestly if you ask directly.
  2. "If you could go back, would you sign again?" The answer to this question is almost always more informative than any answer they give about financials.
  3. "What does the franchisor do well? What do they do poorly?" Support, marketing, supply chain — you want to hear where the gaps are.
  4. "How long did it take you to reach break-even?" Ramp-up time is often dramatically different from what the sales team implies.
  5. "Are you aware of other franchisees who have closed or struggled?" Existing franchisees know things the FDD doesn't show you.

Red Flags That Should Stop You Cold

These are the findings that should give you serious pause — regardless of how enthusiastic you are about the brand:

  • Item 19 missing entirely, or disclosing only top-performing locations
  • Multiple franchisee-initiated lawsuits alleging earnings misrepresentation
  • Annual closure rate above 5%
  • Franchisor financials showing consecutive years of losses with no clear path to profitability
  • Territory language with broad carve-outs for company stores, online sales, or alternative channels
  • Item 17 provisions allowing termination for minor violations without cure periods
  • Franchisee turnover among people in your target market who you can't reach by phone
📋 Pro Tip

The FDD you receive may be a year old. Ask for the most current version — franchisors update annually, and recent litigation or closure events may not appear in an older document. Always verify the issue date on the cover page.

How Long Does FDD Review Take?

A thorough FDD review — reading the key items, calling existing franchisees, and consulting a franchise attorney — typically takes two to four weeks. This is appropriate. You're making a decision that will affect the next 10 years of your life.

If a franchisor is pressuring you to move faster, that's a red flag in itself. Good franchise systems have confidence in their disclosure. They don't need to rush you.

Your 14-day legal waiting period is a floor, not a ceiling. Take the time you need.

Should You Hire a Franchise Attorney?

Yes. Every time. Without exception.

A franchise attorney is not the same as a business attorney. You need someone who specializes in franchise law and understands the nuances of FDD interpretation and franchise agreement negotiation. The Bottom Line

An FDD is not designed to be readable. It's designed to be legally compliant. The information that matters most is buried in Items 3, 12, 17, 19, 20, and 21. Start there.

If you read those six items carefully, call five to ten existing franchisees, and hire a franchise attorney to review the agreement, you will know more about this franchise than 95% of people who sign agreements. That's the work. Most people don't do it. Don't be most people.

The franchise you're evaluating may be a great opportunity. But the only way to know is to read the document.
