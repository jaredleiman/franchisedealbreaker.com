Is Chick-fil-A a Good Franchise to Buy?

Chick-fil-A is one of the most successful quick-service restaurant chains in the United States with over 3,000 locations. Founded in 1967 in College Park, Georgia, the brand is known for its chicken sandwiches, customer service, and Sunday closures.

Chick-fil-A's franchise model is fundamentally different from traditional franchises. The operator fee is only $10,000 — but Chick-fil-A owns all real estate and equipment. Operators receive a share of sales and profits rather than owning a traditional franchise business.

The most common question about Chick-fil-A franchising is whether the low $10,000 entry cost is real. It is — but the tradeoff is that operators don't build equity in a business asset the way traditional franchisees do, and agreements are year-to-year with no guaranteed renewal.

faq

How much does a Chick-fil-A franchise cost?

The operator fee is only $10,000 — but this is not a traditional franchise. Chick-fil-A owns all real estate, equipment, and the business itself. Operators receive approximately 50% of pre-tax profit after paying a 15% royalty. There is no equity ownership and no business to sell.

Has Chick-fil-A been sued by franchisees?

Chick-fil-A has minimal disclosed litigation relative to its system size. The selective operator process and company ownership model reduces the type of franchisee-franchisor disputes common in other systems.

What does Chick-fil-A's Item 19 say about earnings?

Chick-fil-A's average unit volumes are among the highest in QSR — but operators only receive a share of profits, not the full revenue. Net operator income varies but the lack of equity ownership means there is no business asset to sell. This is a critical distinction from traditional franchise ownership.
